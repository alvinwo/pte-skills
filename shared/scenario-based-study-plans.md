# Scenario-Based Study Plans

This file turns score targets into practical preparation strategies.

Use it when the learner does not just ask for a generic PTE plan, but has a concrete outcome such as:
- student visa English requirement
- PR partner pathway / Proficient English ("4个7")
- high-score migration pathway / Superior English ("4个8")

This page is not only about target scores.
It tells the agent how to think about:
- how ambitious the target is
- how much time the learner should spend on each question type
- which low-yield tasks can be deprioritized
- what daily habits should be non-negotiable
- when to shift from foundation training to score-chasing training

## First classify the learner into a scenario
Ask these first:
1. Why are you taking PTE? Student visa, partner visa/PR pathway, points-tested skilled migration, or something else?
2. What exact requirement do you need to meet?
3. What is your deadline?
4. Do you already have a score report? If yes, what are your four communicative skill scores?
5. How many weeks can you study seriously before the test?

Then map the learner to one of the scenarios below.

---

## Scenario 1: Student visa or lower-threshold visa requirement

### Typical user profile
- Needs to clear a minimum threshold, not chase elite scores
- Often only needs a safe pass
- Usually benefits from reducing complexity and avoiding perfectionism

### Coaching principle
The goal is not to master every task equally.
The goal is to pass efficiently and reliably.

### Main study strategy
Prioritize the question types with the best return for the required level.
Do not spend too much time on low-yield reading and listening multiple-choice tasks if the target is modest.

### Recommended priorities
#### First priority
- RA
- RS
- WFD
- DI

#### Second priority
- FIB Reading
- FIB Listening
- HIW
- SWT or WE depending on the learner's weak area

#### Lower priority / can be minimized if time is short
- MCS Reading
- MCM Reading
- MCS Listening
- MCM Listening
- SMW
- HCS

### What to tell this learner
- Do not try to become perfect in every question type.
- Build dependable daily routines on the highest-yield tasks.
- Use templates and safe structures instead of overcomplicated answers.
- If a task gives little score and costs too much time, contain the damage rather than overinvest.

### Time allocation suggestion
If the learner has limited time:
- 50% on RA + RS + WFD
- 20% on DI
- 15% on blanks and HIW
- 15% on writing structure, test strategy, and weak-task cleanup

### Daily habits
- RA every day
- RS every day
- WFD every day
- vocabulary review every day
- short pronunciation and fluency practice every day

## Scenario 2: Proficient English ("4个7") / PR partner pathway

### Typical user profile
- Needs each communicative skill to hit a meaningful threshold
- Can no longer rely only on an overall score
- Must avoid one weak skill dragging the whole result down

### Coaching principle
This learner needs balanced score protection.
High-yield tasks matter most, but weak components must be managed carefully.

### Main study strategy
1. identify the weakest communicative skill first
2. protect the strong skills from dropping
3. push the weakest skill mainly through high-yield integrated tasks
4. deprioritize low-yield question types unless they are specifically causing trouble

### Recommended priorities by skill
#### If Speaking is the risk
- RS
- RA
- DI
- SGD
- RTS
- RL

#### If Listening is the risk
- WFD
- RS
- HIW
- FIB-L
- SGD
- RL

Practical note:
- HIW should usually be tested first for fit
- if the learner can already keep up with the speed, avoid overtraining it
- use the countdown to preview the text, and remember that negative marking means only confident clicks are worth taking

#### If Reading is the risk
- FIB Dropdown
- FIB Drag/Drop
- RO
- SWT
- HIW only after the core reading tasks are stable

#### If Writing is the risk
- WFD
- WE
- SWT
- SST
- spelling and grammar control

### What this learner can sometimes give up
If time is limited and the target is around the threshold rather than elite:
- Reading MCQ can be treated as low priority
- Listening MCQ can be treated as low priority
- SMW and HCS can be controlled but not overtrained

### Time allocation suggestion
- 35% on the learner's weakest skill through high-yield tasks
- 25% on RS + WFD because they support multiple score areas
- 20% on a second weak area
- 20% on maintenance of stronger skills

### Daily habits
- RS every day
- WFD every day
- RA most days
- vocabulary every day
- 1 focused weak-skill block every day
- one timed mixed set several times per week

### Warning sign
A Proficient English ("4个7") learner often fails not because they are bad overall, but because one section stays unstable.
The skill should therefore check section floors, not only total ability.

## Scenario 3: Superior English ("4个8") / 189 or 190 high-score pathway

### Typical user profile
- Needs very strong section scores, not just a pass
- Small weaknesses become expensive
- Needs both efficiency and polish

### Coaching principle
At this level, the learner cannot rely on shortcuts alone.
They still need score-efficient prioritization, but they also need consistency, accuracy, and fatigue control.

### Main study strategy
1. lock in the massive high-yield tasks first
2. raise ceiling and stability in all four communicative skills
3. reduce avoidable errors in spelling, grammar, pronunciation, fluency, and time use
4. optimize lower-yield tasks enough that they do not leak points repeatedly

### Highest-priority engine tasks
- RS
- WFD
- DI
- RA
- SGD
- HIW
- FIB Reading
- FIB Listening

### Important support tasks
- RL
- RTS
- SWT
- WE
- SST
- RO

### Lower priority but should not be ignored completely
- Reading MCQ
- Listening MCQ
- SMW
- HCS

For a Superior English ("4个8") learner, these are not the first place to invest, but they should be trained enough to avoid careless losses.

### Time allocation suggestion
- 30% on RS + WFD
- 20% on DI + RA
- 20% on blanks + HIW
- 15% on writing quality control and integrated writing tasks
- 15% on mock-test review, timing strategy, and low-yield cleanup

### Daily habits
- RS every day
- WFD every day
- RA every day
- DI very frequently
- vocabulary every day
- spelling review every day
- weekly full-section or full-test simulation
- error log review after every serious practice session

### What separates Superior English ("4个8") learners
They need:
- fewer careless spelling mistakes
- cleaner pronunciation and fluency
- better time discipline
- stronger note-to-output conversion in DI, RL, SGD, and SST
- less variance between practice days

---

## Universal habit layer
These habits should appear in almost every plan.

### 1. Vocabulary building
The learner needs a word bank, but not random memorization only.

Recommended vocabulary categories:
- academic connectors
- common topic words for education, technology, society, environment, work, health, government
- high-frequency collocations
- spelling-sensitive words for WFD and SST
- pronunciation-sensitive words for RA and RS

### 2. Speaking every day
Especially for:
- RA
- RS
- DI

At minimum, a serious learner should practice:
- reading clearly and steadily
- speaking without long pauses
- chunking sentences naturally
- maintaining volume and rhythm

### 3. Listening every day
Especially through:
- WFD
- RS
- FIB-L
- HIW

This improves:
- sound discrimination
- spelling recall
- memory span
- phrase chunking
- speed of note-to-answer conversion

### 4. Error logging
The learner should track repeated errors such as:
- dropped plural endings
- article mistakes
- weak spelling patterns
- unstable pronunciation of specific sounds
- repeating the same DI structure without content
- missing function words in WFD

### 5. Time management
The skill should teach not just question mastery, but time economy.
This matters especially for:
- reading section decision-making
- SWT and WE timing
- when to skip or cap effort on low-yield tasks

## Preparation timeline framework
Use this to decide when to prepare what.

### Phase 1: Foundation phase
Best when the test is still weeks away or the learner is clearly below target.

Focus on:
- vocabulary building
- pronunciation and fluency basics
- daily RS and WFD habits
- understanding question formats
- safe templates for DI, SWT, WE, and RTS
- accuracy before speed in blanks and dictation
- heavy early effort on listening and speaking engine tasks before later writing polish

### Phase 2: Score-building phase
Use when the learner understands the tasks but is not yet consistently hitting target.

Focus on:
- the highest-yield tasks for the learner's target scenario
- weak-skill routing
- timed practice sets
- error pattern reduction
- strategy for low-yield tasks: contain loss, don't waste energy

### Phase 3: Exam-shaping phase
Use in the last 1-2 weeks before the exam.

Focus on:
- stability, not experimentation
- full-section or mixed mocks
- pacing decisions
- fatigue management
- reducing careless mistakes
- reinforcing the learner's strongest dependable templates and routines

## Runtime coaching rule
When the learner gives a target, the agent should not stop at a score threshold.
It should answer all four:
1. what to prepare first
2. how to prepare it
3. what can be deprioritized
4. what daily habits should be non-negotiable

If the learner has a real exam date and cares about score certainty, the agent should also say whether an official PTE mock is recommended as the final validation layer.
