# Low-Yield vs High-Yield Questions

This file helps the agent explain where learners should invest effort and where they can safely spend less time.

Use it when the learner asks:
- which question types matter most?
- what can I skip?
- what should I focus on first?
- is this question type worth my time?

This file should be used together with:
- `shared/current-score-priority-matrix.md`
- `shared/scenario-based-study-plans.md`
- `shared/target-score-planning.md`

## Core principle
Not all question types deserve equal study time.

The right investment depends on:
1. the learner's target scenario
2. the learner's weak communicative skill
3. how much score a task can realistically influence
4. how difficult the task is for the learner

A question type can be low-yield because:
- it contributes little to the score
- it appears infrequently
- it costs too much time to improve
- it does not transfer strongly to other tasks

A question type can be high-yield because:
- it contributes to multiple communicative skills
- it appears often
- it supports other tasks indirectly
- it gives strong return on daily practice

---

## Tier 1: Highest-yield engine tasks
These are the default first priorities for many learners.

### RS
Why it is high-yield:
- supports Speaking and Listening
- frequent in the exam
- trains memory, chunking, and fluency
- daily practice often gives visible return

### WFD
Why it is high-yield:
- supports Listening and Writing
- high score value in practical matrices
- improves spelling, listening precision, and function-word awareness

### RA
Why it is high-yield:
- strong Speaking value
- improves pronunciation and fluency
- helps microphone confidence

### DI
Why it is high-yield:
- large Speaking contribution in current practical matrix
- can improve quickly with safe structure and repetition

### Reading / Listening blanks
Why they are high-yield:
- direct scoring
- strong return from grammar, collocation, and attention training
- often more valuable than multiple-choice tasks

### HIW
Why it is often high-yield:
- useful Listening and Reading crossover
- strong accuracy training
- supports concentration and word recognition

---

## Tier 2: Important support tasks
These tasks matter, but are often not the first place to invest all study time.

### RL
Useful because it supports Speaking and Listening, but it is harder and usually less efficient than RS or DI for many learners.

### SGD
Important in the current test structure and should not be ignored, but many learners should first stabilize RS, WFD, RA, and DI.

### RTS
Relevant for Speaking and now part of the current format, but not usually the first engine task.

### SWT
Useful for Reading and Writing, especially when a learner needs balanced written accuracy.

### WE
Important for Writing, especially when writing is weak, but not always the fastest score-gain task compared with integrated tasks like WFD.

### SST
Useful for Listening + Writing, but usually more demanding and lower-frequency than engine tasks.

### RO
Important enough for Reading learners, but still usually behind blanks in priority.

---

## Tier 3: Usually low-yield tasks
These tasks are not worthless, but often deserve less study time.

### Reading MCQ
- MCM Reading
- MCS Reading

Why often low-yield:
- lower practical weighting
- improvement is often slower
- can consume too much time in reading section strategy

### Listening MCQ
- MCM Listening
- MCS Listening

Why often low-yield:
- limited score return
- can be time-consuming
- difficult to improve quickly compared with RS, WFD, HIW, and FIB-L

### SMW
Usually a minor task with limited score effect.

### HCS
Can matter somewhat, but often should not be overtrained compared with WFD, HIW, and FIB-L.

## Scenario-specific guidance

## Student visa / lower-threshold learner
### Recommended approach
Go hard on:
- RA
- RS
- WFD
- DI
- basic blanks

Reduce time on:
- reading MCQ
- listening MCQ
- SMW
- HCS

### Coaching message
Your job is not to win every question.
Your job is to pass efficiently.

## all four communicative skills at 65+ learner
### Recommended approach
Focus on:
- RS
- WFD
- RA
- DI
- blanks
- the weak-skill shortlist

Reduce time on:
- reading MCQ unless reading is clearly unstable
- listening MCQ unless listening is specifically weak
- minor low-return tasks if the main section floor is not yet stable

### Coaching message
Protect every section. Do not let low-yield tasks steal time from the tasks that raise the weak score.

## all four communicative skills at 79+ learner
### Recommended approach
Still prioritize engine tasks first, but do not fully ignore lower-yield tasks.

Main investment:
- RS
- WFD
- RA
- DI
- blanks
- HIW

Secondary cleanup:
- RL
- SGD
- RTS
- WE
- SWT
- SST

Leak prevention:
- reading MCQ
- listening MCQ
- SMW
- HCS

### Coaching message
At this level, even low-yield tasks can matter if they leak points repeatedly. Prioritize them later, not never.

## How the agent should answer "what can I skip?"
The answer should never be a raw list only.
It should explain three things:
1. what to prioritize
2. what can be deprioritized
3. what minimum competence still needs to be maintained

### Good answer pattern
- "For your target, focus first on RS, WFD, RA, and DI."
- "You can spend much less time on reading MCQ and listening MCQ for now."
- "Do a small maintenance amount so these tasks do not become complete disasters, but do not let them replace your high-yield work."

---

## Red flags for deprioritization
A learner should **not** deprioritize a task if:
- it is their obvious weak area and is dragging down a required section
- they are making major time-management errors on it
- the task is causing panic that spills into later questions
- the learner is targeting very high scores and keeps losing easy points there

---

## Coaching rule
The skill should avoid two bad extremes:
- telling everyone to practice everything equally
- telling everyone to completely skip the same tasks

Instead, it should say:
- for your target
- for your weakest skill
- with your available study time
- here is the smartest allocation of effort
